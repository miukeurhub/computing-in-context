Deforestation and Food Security#

Deforestation, driven by industrialization and agricultural expansion, directly impacts global food security. Approximately 40% of the resources used in the food industry rely on nature, including forests, which provide critical ecosystem services essential for sustainable agriculture. Forests regulate rainfall, prevent soil erosion, support biodiversity (like pollinators and pest controllers), and stabilize the climate. Their destruction disrupts food supply chains, decreases crop yields, and reduces dietary diversity, particularly in regions heavily reliant on forest ecosystems. These cascading effects highlight the urgent need to understand and address the relationship between deforestation and food security.

  1. Research Question:
    What is the relationship between deforestation and the Global Food Security Index across key sub-regions from 2012 to 2022?

  2. Hypothesis:
    Increasing rates of deforestation negatively impact the Global Food Security Index by:

    • Reducing agricultural productivity.

    • Disrupting food supply chains.

    • Decreasing dietary diversity.

These effects are expected to vary significantly across sub-regions, with tropical regions experiencing greater vulnerabilities compared to temperate regions.

According to ISO 3166-1, codes starting with ‘X’ are reserved for user-assigned purposes and do not officially represent recognized countries. However, these codes are often used informally in datasets and applications to denote specific regions or entities. Here’s how the following codes are interpreted:

  • XAD: Commonly used to denote Andorra.

  • XKO: Typically used to represent Kosovo.

  • XNC: Frequently stands for New Caledonia.

The code XCA appears incomplete or undocumented. As a result, I have decided to drop it from the analysis.

Step 5 - Exploratory Data Analysis#

Let’s begin by analyzing the trends in both deforestation and the Food Security Index (FSI) across different sub-regions. This will help us uncover nuances and identify specific patterns that might explain the relationship between tree cover loss and food security. By breaking it down region by region, we can better understand the variations and unique factors influencing these trends globally.

deforest_agg = data.groupby(['year', 'continent_sub_region']).agg(
    {'umd_tree_cover_loss__ha': 'sum', 'fsi': 'mean'}
).reset_index()

fig = px.line(
    deforest_agg,
    x="year", 
    y="umd_tree_cover_loss__ha", 
    color="continent_sub_region",
    labels={"year": "Year",
        "umd_tree_cover_loss__ha": "Tree Cover Loss (ha)",
        "continent_region": "Continent",
    },
    title="Deforestation by Region Over Time"
)
fig.show()

Deforestation by Region Over Time#

From this graph, we can see that tree cover loss varies widely across regions from 2012 to 2022. Latin America and the Caribbean, along with Sub-Saharan Africa, have the highest levels of loss. Latin America experiences sharp spikes, particularly around 2015-2017, likely driven by activities such as farming and logging. Meanwhile, Sub-Saharan Africa shows a steady rise, reflecting ongoing pressures like expanding farmland and resource use.

In contrast, regions like Northern and Western Europe, and Australia and New Zealand, have minimal tree cover loss, suggesting stronger forest protection measures. Eastern and Southeastern Asia display a gradual decline in tree cover loss, possibly due to reforestation efforts and stricter environmental policies. This graph highlights that different regions face unique challenges, emphasizing the need for tailored strategies to address deforestation effectively.

From this map, we can see clear patterns of tree cover loss across different regions of the world between 2012 and 2022. This reflects the pressures from agricultural expansion, logging, and infrastructure development, which continue to challenge conservation efforts in one of the planet’s most biodiverse regions.

This may reflect more established forest management practices and stronger regulatory frameworks. However, these regions are not entirely immune to the pressures of urbanization and industrial development.

Meanwhile, parts of Africa reveal a mixed story. Some countries show minimal tree cover loss, while others are heavily impacted by land clearing for agriculture and charcoal production, highlighting the ongoing struggles of balancing environmental and livelihood needs.

Overall, this map underscores the importance of global efforts to mitigate deforestation, addressing its root causes while promoting sustainable development. It also serves as a visual reminder of the varying degrees of progress and challenges faced by different regions in protecting their natural resources.

Conclusion#

After conducting the data analysis, we found that the relationship between deforestation and the Global Food Security Index (GFSI) varies significantly across different sub-regions. Here’s what the results tell us:

  • Deforestation Impacts Agricultural Productivity: In regions like Sub-Saharan Africa and South America, where agriculture heavily depends on natural forest ecosystems, deforestation has a clear negative effect on food security. The loss of forests disrupts soil fertility, water availability, and other environmental conditions crucial for farming.

  • Food Supply Chains Are Affected Unevenly: In regions like Southeast Asia and Latin America, deforestation appears to impact food supply chains more noticeably. However, in areas with robust infrastructure and resources, such as Northern Europe or North America, the effects are less pronounced, likely because these regions have better systems to adapt and maintain food security.

  • Dietary Diversity Suffers in Vulnerable Regions: Regions where forests are a direct source of food, like parts of Asia and Central Africa, show reduced dietary diversity due to deforestation. In contrast, wealthier or industrialized regions are less impacted, as they have alternative food sources.

What This Means:

The analysis confirms that deforestation plays a role in shaping food security, but the impact is far from uniform. Regions that depend heavily on forests for farming and food supply are the most vulnerable, while those with stronger systems and alternative resources are more resilient. These findings highlight the need for tailored strategies to address deforestation’s effects on food security in different parts of the world.
